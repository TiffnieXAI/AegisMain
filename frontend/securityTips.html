Security Tips

Essential knowledge for staying safe in the blockchain ecosystem

Grandma Test

A.E.G.I.S. is designed to pass the "Grandma Test" - meaning our security warnings and explanations are clear enough that your grandmother could understand them. We believe security shouldn't require a computer science degree.

Remember: In Web3, there's no "undo button." Once a transaction is signed, it's permanent. That's why A.E.G.I.S. exists - to give you confidence and clarity before that final click.

Security Best Practices

Never Trust Unlimited Approvals

Legitimate dApps only request specific token amounts. If a contract asks for unlimited approval (uint256.max), it's likely a scam trying to drain your wallet.

Verify Contract Addresses

Scammers create fake websites with lookalike domains. Always verify the contract address on official sources like the project's verified Twitter or Discord.

Understand What You're Signing

Never 'blind sign' transactions you don't understand. A.E.G.I.S. translates technical data into plain English so you always know what you're approving.

Research Before Investing

Check if contracts are audited, read community reviews, and verify team credentials. High returns often come with high risks or are outright scams.

Beware of Urgency Tactics

Scammers create false urgency ('Mint ends in 1 hour!'). Legitimate projects give ample time. Never rush into transactions due to FOMO.

Use Hardware Wallets

For significant holdings, use hardware wallets like Ledger or Trezor. They provide an extra layer of protection by keeping your private keys offline.

Watch for Honeypot Contracts

Some malicious contracts let you buy tokens but prevent selling. A.E.G.I.S. simulates transactions to detect these traps before you lose funds.

Regular Security Audits

Periodically review and revoke unnecessary token approvals. Use tools like Revoke.cash to manage permissions you've granted to contracts.

Common Web3 Threats

Phishing Attacks

Fake websites and contracts designed to steal your assets

Common Examples:

  • Lookalike domain names
  • Cloned dApp interfaces
  • Fake airdrop sites

How to Protect:

  • Verify URLs carefully
  • Use bookmarks for official sites
  • Check Contract Addresses

Unlimited Approvals

Malicious contracts requesting full access to your tokens

Common Examples:

  • uint256.max approval requests
  • Unnecessary permission grants
  • Hidden approval functions

How to Protect:

  • Only approve specific amounts
  • Regularly revoke old approvals
  • Question why unlimited access is needed

Rug Pulls

Projects that drain liquidity and disappear

Common Examples:

  • Unlocked liquidity pools
  • Anonymous team members
  • No vesting periods

How to Protect:

  • Research team credentials
  • Check liquidity locks
  • Verify tokenomics

Honeypot Contracts

Tokens you can buy but can never sell

Common Examples:

  • Modified transfer functions
  • Hidden selling restrictions
  • Blacklist mechanisms

How to Protect:

  • Check contract code before buying
  • Look for audit reports
  • Test with small amounts first

Key Takeaways

  • Transaction Permanence: Blockchain transactions cannot be reversed. Always verify before signing.
  • Trust Verification: Don't trust, verify. Check contracts, team credentials, and audit reports before interacting.
  • Security Tools: Use A.E.G.I.S. and similar tools to analyze transactions before signing. Prevention is better than cure.
  • Community Knowledge: Learn from others' mistakes. Follow security researchers and stay updated on new threats.
